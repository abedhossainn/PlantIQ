## Ignition and Auto Ignition

An often-unexpected hazard is ignition from flames or sparks.  Consequently, LNG facilities are designed and operated using standards and procedures to eliminate this hazard, and are equipped with extensive fire detection and protection systems should flames or sparks occur.

The auto ignition temperature is the lowest temperature at which a flammable gas vapor will ignite spontaneously, without a source of ignition, after several minutes of exposure to sources of heat.

- Temperatures higher than the auto ignition temperature will cause ignition after a shorter exposure time.
- With exceedingly hot temperatures, and within the flammability range, ignition can be virtually instantaneous.

For methane vapors derived from LNG, with a fuel-air mixture of 10% methane in air (about the middle of the 5 -15% flammability limit) and atmospheric pressure, the auto ignition temperature is above 1,000°F.  This extremely hot temperature requires a strong source of thermal radiation, heat, or hot surface.

If LNG is spilled on the ground or on water and the resulting flammable gas vapor does not encounter an ignition source (a flame or spark or a source of heat &gt;1,000°F), the vapor will dissipate into the atmosphere, and no fire will take place.

When compared to other fuels, LNG vapor (methane) requires the highest temperature for auto ignition as shown in the following table:

|Fuel|Auto-Ignition Temperature (°F)|
|-------------------------|----------------------------------|
|LNG (primarily methane)|1,004|
|LPG|850 - 950|
|Ethanol|780 - 800|
|Methanol (Methane)|867|
|Gasoline|495|
|Diesel Fuel|Approx. 450|
