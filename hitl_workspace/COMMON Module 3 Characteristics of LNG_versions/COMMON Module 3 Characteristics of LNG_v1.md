## MODULE 3 OVERVIEW

This self-study module is designed to equip Operations Field Technicians with the knowledge and familiarization of the properties associated with LNG, how to be prepared to recognize process hazards, how LNG can produce cryogenic burns, how to handle cryogenic liquids, and how to wear the proper personal protective equipment (PPE).

The possibility of large spills of liquefied natural gas (LNG) from large, insulated storage tanks or ocean-going LNG tank ships has received increased attention as the demand for natural gas has increased.  This attention has resulted because:

- LNG has hazardous properties,
- LNG is stored at extremely low temperatures in large, insulated storage tanks, and
- LNG is transported in large, highly visible ships.

LNG spills are considered serious events because the spill surface provides a large heat source that will rapidly vaporize the LNG. The resultant flammable vapor cloud can travel downwind before diluting to safe concentrations. If the flammable vapor is ignited, a vapor cloud fire followed by a pool fire can occur.

## Physical Properties of Hydrocarbons

The properties of each type of hydrocarbon depend on the number of carbon and hydrogen atoms in the hydrocarbon molecules, and how they are joined.

Hydrocarbon is the name given to the large family of chemical compounds made up entirely of hydrogen and carbon.  Crude oil is a mixture composed almost entirely of hydrocarbons, with a few other compounds like hydrogen sulfide (H2S) present in some cases.

The normal boiling point is 'the temperature at which a liquid boils at atmospheric pressure.'  The boiling points of hydrocarbons are related to the number of carbon atoms. The more carbon atoms, the higher the boiling point. The difference in boiling points makes possible the separation of different hydrocarbons (distillation and fractionation).

The boiling point of a compound is affected by the pressure at which the compound is held, e.g., propane boils at -43.8°F @ atmospheric pressure; however, if contained in a system at about 174 psi, the boiling point rises to +95°F.

Any liquid is made up of many molecules, all of which are in motion. Some of these molecules have enough energy to break through the liquid's surface into the gaseous state. Consider an open container partially filled with liquid.

- The molecules that break free of the liquid can escape completely and the liquid will eventually evaporate.
- However, in a closed container, the increasing number of gaseous molecules creates an increase in pressure. It also increases the tendency for the molecules to re-enter the liquid.

Equilibrium is reached when the number of molecules leaving the liquid state equals the number re-entering the liquid. The pressure developed at this equilibrium is known as the vapor pressure of the liquid.

## Vapor Pressure and Temperature / Boiling Point and Vapor Pressure / Specific Gravity

The relationship between vapor pressure and temperature can be understood in terms of molecules in the liquid escaping the attractive forces of the liquid to enter the gas phase.

When a liquid is heated in an open vessel, the vapor pressure will increase until it reaches the atmospheric pressure surrounding the liquid. When this happens, it is accompanied by the physical phenomena of boiling, as vapor bubbles form in the liquid and rise to the surface.

The boiling point of a liquid is 'the temperature at which its vapor pressure is equal to the surrounding pressure.'

Specific gravity of a liquid is 'a ratio of the weight of a volume of the liquid to the weight of an equal volume of water at a designated temperature.'

Specific gravity of a gas is 'a ratio of the weight of a volume of the gas to the weight of an equal volume of air at a designated temperature and pressure.'

## States of Matter

All matter exists in one of three states: solid, liquid, or a gas (refer to Figure 4 ).

1. In a solid , molecules are close together and are slow-moving.
2. In a liquid , molecules are further apart and move faster than in a solid.
3. In a gas , molecules are far apart and move very rapidly.

The rate of molecule movement determines whether matter is a solid, a liquid, or a gas.

## Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) Properties

Natural gas produced from the wellhead consists of methane, ethane, propane, and heavier hydrocarbons, plus small quantities of nitrogen, helium, carbon dioxide, sulfur compounds, and water. LNG is liquefied natural gas .



**[Figure 33: The image displays a learning objective labeled "Learning Objective 5," which instructs the reader to describe the key definitions of the characteristics of liquefied natural gas (LNG), emphasizing the need for an accurate and comprehensive understanding of its properties and attributes.]**



The LNG Liquefaction process first requires pre-treatment of the natural gas stream to remove impurities such as mercury, water, carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulfide, and other sulfur compounds. By removing these impurities, solids cannot be formed while the gas is refrigerated.  The final product will then meet the quality specifications of the LNG end users.

Natural gas is liquefied at high pressure and warmer temperature than -256 o F.  Then, its pressure is reduced to be stored slightly above atmospheric pressure and below -256 o F.  LNG takes up only 1/600th of the volume required for a comparable amount of natural gas at room temperature and normal atmospheric pressure.  Because it is not stored under pressure, the common misperception of LNG as a pressurized substance has led to an erroneous understanding of its danger.

LNG is a clear, non-corrosive, non-toxic, near odorless, cryogenic liquid at normal atmospheric pressure.  Although methane is non-toxic, as with any gaseous material besides air and oxygen, natural gas that is vaporized from LNG can cause asphyxiation due to lack of oxygen if a concentration of gas develops in an unventilated, confined area.

## Understanding LNG Fire Hazards

Vapors released from LNG as it returns to a gas phase, if not properly and safely managed, can become flammable, but explosive only under certain conditions.  Safety and security measures contained in the engineering design and technologies, and in the operating procedures of LNG facilities reduce these potential dangers.

## Flammability Range

The flammability range is ' the range between the minimum and maximum concentrations of vapor (percent by volume) in which air and LNG vapors form a flammable mixture that can be ignited and burn. '

Figure 7 indicates that the Upper Flammability Limit (UFL) and Lower Flammability Limit (LFL) of Methane, the dominant component of LNG vapor, are 5% and 15% by volume, respectively.

- When fuel concentration exceeds its UFL, it cannot burn because too little oxygen is present.

This situation exists, for example, in a closed, secure storage tank where the vapor concentration is near 100% methane.

- When fuel concentration is below the LFL, it cannot burn because  too little methane is present.  An example is leakage of small quantities of LNG in a well-ventilated area.

In this situation, the LNG vapor will rapidly mix with air and dissipate to less than 5% concentration.

## Ignition and Auto Ignition

An often-unexpected hazard is ignition from flames or sparks.  Consequently, LNG facilities are designed and operated using standards and procedures to eliminate this hazard, and are equipped with extensive fire detection and protection systems should flames or sparks occur.

The auto ignition temperature is the lowest temperature at which a flammable gas vapor will ignite spontaneously, without a source of ignition, after several minutes of exposure to sources of heat.

- Temperatures higher than the auto ignition temperature will cause ignition after a shorter exposure time.
- With exceedingly hot temperatures, and within the flammability range, ignition can be virtually instantaneous.

For methane vapors derived from LNG, with a fuel-air mixture of 10% methane in air (about the middle of the 5 -15% flammability limit) and atmospheric pressure, the auto ignition temperature is above 1,000°F.  This extremely hot temperature requires a strong source of thermal radiation, heat, or hot surface.

If LNG is spilled on the ground or on water and the resulting flammable gas vapor does not encounter an ignition source (a flame or spark or a source of heat &gt;1,000°F), the vapor will dissipate into the atmosphere, and no fire will take place.

When compared to other fuels, LNG vapor (methane) requires the highest temperature for auto ignition as shown in the following table:

|Fuel|Auto-Ignition Temperature (°F)|
|-------------------------|----------------------------------|
|LNG (primarily methane)|1,004|
|LPG|850 - 950|
|Ethanol|780 - 800|
|Methanol (Methane)|867|
|Gasoline|495|
|Diesel Fuel|Approx. 450|

## Potential Hazards

Potential hazards resulting from intentional or accidental spilling of large quantities of LNG include thermal radiation from vapor cloud fires (also referred to as flash fires ) and pool fires .  There is general agreement among LNG experts regarding the following aspects of potential LNG fire and explosion hazards:

Vapors from large, unignited spills of LNG cannot travel far into developed areas without finding an ignition source, igniting, and burning back to the source.

## Vapor Cloud Fire

Once delayed ignition of the vapor cloud occurs, and provided that the cloud is unconfined and rich in methane, the LNG vapor will burn in the form of a vapor cloud fire .



**[Figure 39: The image depicts a standard graphical icon representing a document or file, characterized by a white rectangular shape with rounded corners, a blue vertical stripe on the left side, and a small triangular tab at the top right corner, all set against a transparent background, commonly used in user interfaces to symbolize text-based files such as documents or reports.]**



## NOTE

A vapor cloud fire , also called 'flash fire,' refers to the combustion of a vapor cloud without generating any significant overpressure.

A vapor cloud traversing over commercial and/or residential terrain will almost certainly encounter an ignition source early in its downwind drift, and the resulting vapor cloud fire will burn back to the source.

The vapor cloud fire will burn back to the source and cause a pool fire at the source if the release is a continuous release, and the release duration is longer than the time it takes the cloud to find an ignition source.

If the vapor cloud is confined and/or the vapors contain large amounts of heavier hydrocarbons (C2+, ethane, propane, butane, etc.), then the flame can accelerate and result in an explosion.  The magnitude of the explosion and explosion damage depends on several factors including the amount of vapors above the lower flammable limit, the presence of obstacles and degree of confinement, the composition of the vapor cloud, and the strength of the ignition source.

## Pool Fire

If immediate ignition occurs, a pool fire will result.  The extent of the pool spreading (diameter), and flame height will depend on several factors including the flow rate of LNG, the spill surface type (water or land), the spill surface geometry, spill surface roughness, release composition, release temperature, ambient wind speed, ambient temperature, ambient relative humidity, and containment pit or bounded area.



**[Figure 40: The image depicts a standard graphical icon representing a document or file, characterized by a white rectangular shape with rounded corners, a blue triangular arrow pointing upward on the left side, and three horizontal lines stacked vertically within the rectangle, symbolizing text or content, commonly used in user interfaces to denote a generic file or document type.]**



## NOTE

A pool fire is a type of diffusion flame where a layer of volatile liquid fuel is evaporating and burning.

If the liquid pool is unconfined and the inventory of LNG is large, the fire will continue to burn until all the fuel is exhausted by the pool fire.  It is not practical or even possible to extinguish large LNG pool fires resulting from large spills of LNG unless the flow of LNG feeding the pool can be stopped.

## Calculating Who is at Risk

The maximum vapor cloud fire hazard area from large LNG spills is typically estimated by calculating a downwind dispersion distance to the lower flammable limit (LFL) and a crosswind dispersion distance to 1/2 LFL at low wind speed and stable atmospheric conditions.  This maximum fire hazard zone is very unlikely to be experienced in any situation where the cloud drifts overpopulated areas. As indicated above, the cloud will soon encounter an ignition source and burn back to the source well before the maximum hazard area is reached.

Only the outdoor population present within the flammable boundaries of the vapor cloud is assumed to be injured due to (a) short exposure to very high thermal radiation fluxes from the vapor cloud fire, (b) direct flame contact, (c) secondary fires of clothing, and (d) inhalation of hot combustion products.

- It is assumed that people inside buildings at the time of the flash fire will not be injured.
- It is also assumed that people inside buildings ignited by flash fire or pool fire will escape from the burning structure without direct thermal impact injuries.  This is because the flash fire will ignite buildings from the outside and it will take some time for the fires to penetrate inside.

## Fire Hazards Summary

Questions about LNG safety often demonstrate how LNG is often confused with other fuels and materials.  All these fuels can be used safely if proper safety, security, and environmental protections are in place.  We fill our cars and trucks with gasoline, use LPG (propane) in our backyard grills, and use methane to heat our homes, hundreds of millions of times each day, and serious safety incidents are rare.

In summary, LNG is an extremely cold, non-toxic, non-corrosive substance that is transferred and stored at atmospheric pressure.  It is refrigerated, rather than pressurized, which enables LNG to be an effective, economical method of transporting large volumes of natural gas over long distances. LNG poses little danger if it is contained in storage tanks, piping, and equipment designed for use in LNG cryogenic conditions.  However, vapors resulting from LNG because of an uncontrolled release can be hazardous, within the key properties of LNG and LNG vapors -flammability range and in contact with a source of ignition -as described above.

## Explosion

An explosion happens when a substance rapidly changes its chemical state, i.e., is ignited or is uncontrollably released from a pressurized state.  For an uncontrolled release to happen, there must be a structural failure (ruptured pipe, tank, pump, etc.). LNG tanks store the liquid at an extremely low temperature, about -256°F, so no pressure is required to maintain its liquid state.  Since LNG is stored at atmospheric pressure, a crack or puncture in the container will not create an immediate explosion.

## Vapor Clouds

As LNG leaves a temperature-controlled container, it begins to warm up, returning the liquid to a gaseous state.  Initially, the gas is colder and heavier than the surrounding air.  It creates a fog -a vapor cloud -above the released liquid.  As the gas warms, it mixes with the surrounding air and begins to disperse.  The vapor cloud will only ignite if it encounters an ignition source while concentrated within its flammability range.

## Cryogenic Burns

The effects of cryogenic liquid upon the skin are very rapid and can cause frostbite and freezing. The injury received can be severe, can occur in seconds, and has the same impact as a burn injury, 1st, 2nd, or 3rd degree.  This must be kept in mind when responding to LNG or Liquid Nitrogen (LN2) spills.

## PPE

The adverse effect of a cryogenic liquid upon the skin can be minimized by wearing the proper personal protective equipment (PPE).  These items include, but are not limited to:

- Rain slickers
- Face shields and goggles
- Hard hat
- Rubber coated gloves
- Rubber boots

## Severity Identification

The following are general indications and identification of the severity of a cryogenic burn:

- 1st degree burns can cause numbing of the skin, which may turn white in color. Skin may feel stiff to the touch, but underlying tissue is still soft.
- 2nd degree burns can cause skin to turn white or blue in color and feel hard and frozen. The underlying tissue is still undamaged; blistering is likely.
- 3rd degree burns can cause skin to be white, blotchy, and/or blue in color. The underlying tissue is hard and cold to the touch.

## Emergency Action Steps

The following steps should be taken immediately to treat cryogenic burns:

1. Immediately flush the affected areas with clean, cool water.
2. Wrap the affected areas with loose, clean bandages.
3. DO NOT rub the affected areas.
4. Seek immediate medical assistance .

## Handling Cryogenic Liquids

Most cryogenic liquids are odorless, colorless, and tasteless when vaporized.  When cryogenic liquids are exposed to the atmosphere, the cold boil-off gases condense the moisture in the air, creating a highly visible fog.

ALWAYS handle these liquids carefully. Because of their extremely low temperatures, they can produce cryogenic burns and frostbite.  When spilled on a surface, they tend to cover it completely and, therefore, cool a large area.  The vapors from these liquids are also extremely cold and can produce burns.  Exposure that may be too brief to affect the skin of the face or hands may damage delicate tissues, such as the eyes.

Boiling and splashing always occurs when charging or filling a warm container with cryogenic liquid or when inserting objects into these liquids.  Perform these tasks slowly to minimize boiling and splashing.  Use tongs to withdraw objects immersed in a cryogenic liquid.

NEVER touch uninsulated pipes or vessels containing cryogenic liquids.  Flesh will stick to extremely cold materials.  Even non-metallic materials are dangerous to touch at low temperatures.  In addition to the hazards of frostbite or flesh sticking to cold materials, objects that are soft and pliable at room temperature, such as rubber or plastic, become hard and brittle, and break easily at these extremely low temperatures.

## Rollover

When LNG supplies of multiple densities (various places of origin, age, etc.) are loaded into a tank one at a time, they do not initially mix.  Instead, they will layer themselves in unstable strata within the tank.  As the lower LNG layer is heated by normal heat leak, it changes density until it finally becomes lighter than the upper layer.  At that point, a liquid rollover will occur, which can cause a sudden increase in vapor generation within the tank.  It is possible to experience a rollover so violent as to generate more vapor than the tank's pressure control devices (PSV's, vents, boil -off gas compression) can handle, which can cause cracks or other structural failures in the tank.

To help prevent the layering, or stratification, the operators unloading an LNG vessel, or simply consolidating inventory, monitor the density of both the product being transferred and the product in the receiving tank, and decide whether to fill the receiving tank from the bottom or the top fill connections.

## Rapid Phase Transition

When released on water, LNG floats and vaporizes.  If large volumes of LNG are released on water, it may vaporize too quickly, causing a rapid phase transition (RPT) or physical explosion.

The same effect can happen when large amounts of water are introduced to an LNG pool.  Water temperature and the presence of substances other than methane also affect the likelihood of an RPT. An RPT can only occur if there is mixing between the LNG and water.  A common example of an RPT is the rapid flash to steam when water is added to a pan of hot oil.
