## What are hydrocarbons and how are they structured?

Hydrocarbons are a large family of chemical compounds composed entirely of the elements hydrogen and carbon. In a hydrocarbon molecule, carbon and hydrogen atoms are joined by chemical bonds. A fundamental rule of hydrocarbon chemistry is that every carbon atom has four "arms" (valence bonds) that must be occupied by either a hydrogen atom, another carbon atom, or an atom of a different element. Hydrogen atoms behave as if they have one "arm."

Common hydrocarbons found in Natural Gas include:
- **Methane (CH4)**: The simplest paraffin; 1 carbon, 4 hydrogens.
- **Ethane (C2H6)**: Formed by adding 1 carbon and 2 hydrogens to methane.
- **Propane (C3H8)**: Formed by adding another carbon and 2 hydrogens.
- **Butane (C4H10)**: Composed of 4 carbon atoms.
- **Pentane**: Heavier hydrocarbon found in fuel gas.

## What are the physical properties of hydrocarbons regarding boiling and pressure?

The normal boiling point is the temperature at which a liquid boils at atmospheric pressure. Boiling points are related to the number of carbon atoms; more carbon atoms result in a higher boiling point. Pressure also affects the boiling point: for example, propane boils at **-43.8°F** at atmospheric pressure, but its boiling point rises to **+95°F** when contained at **174 psi**.

Specific gravity for a liquid is a ratio of its weight to an equal volume of water at a designated temperature. Specific gravity for a gas is a ratio to the weight of an equal volume of air.

## What are the Ideal Gas Laws and standard calculation conditions?

In hydrocarbon processing, all gases are contained in pipelines and vessels. To remove the effects of temperature and pressure on volume, all calculations are made assuming a **standard temperature of 60°F** and a **standard pressure of 14.7 psia** (air pressure at sea level). The property changes of a gas are explained by the Ideal Gas Laws:

- **Boyle's Law**: States that for any isothermal (same temperature) process, at constant temperature, if the volume of a gas is reduced by half, its pressure is doubled. Formula: $P \alpha 1/V$ or $PV = \text{constant}$.
- **Charles' Law**: States that for any isobaric (same pressure) process, at the same pressure, if the absolute temperature of a gas is increased by double, its volume will also be doubled. Formula: $V/T = \text{constant}$.
- **Combined Gas Law**: Relates the three variables of pressure, temperature, and volume simultaneously: $(P_1 \times V_1) / T_1 = (P_2 \times V_2) / T_2$.
- **Density**: The mass per unit volume measured in pounds per cubic foot at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP) of **60°F** and **14.7 psi**.
- **Compressibility**: The ability to be compressed in volume. Compressing a gas reduces its volume by squeezing the gas molecules together, thereby making it hotter.
- **Inertness**: The property whereby a gas (such as Nitrogen) does not react chemically with other matter. It is used to remove air or hydrocarbons from equipment and is not flammable under normal conditions.

## What are the flammability limits of different hydrocarbons?

The flammability range for **Methane (C1)** is strictly between **5% (LFL)** and **15% (UFL)**. If concentration is below 5%, it is too lean to burn; if above 15%, it is too rich to burn.

### Flammability Limits of Various Compounds (Figure 3)
- **Methane (C1)**: LEL 4.4% | UEL 16.5% (Note: Manual general text uses 5-15% for LNG purposes)
- **Ethane (C2)**: LEL 3.0% | UEL 12.5%
- **Propane (C3)**: LEL 2.1% | UEL 9.5%
- **Butane (C4)**: LEL 1.9% | UEL 8.5%
- **Pentane (C5)**: LEL 1.5% | UEL 7.8%
- **Hydrogen (H2)**: LEL 4.1% | UEL 74.0%
- **Acetylene**: LEL 1.5% | UEL 99.0%

## What are the specific indications of cryogenic burn severity?

Cryogenic burns from LNG (**-256°F**) or Liquid Nitrogen can cause rapid injury that mirrors thermal burn degrees.

**Severity Identification**:
- **1st Degree Burn**: Causes numbing of the skin; skin may turn white. Skin feels stiff to the touch, but the underlying tissue is still soft.
- **2nd Degree Burn**: Skin turns white or blue and feels hard and frozen. The underlying tissue is undamaged, but blistering is likely to occur.
- **3rd Degree Burn**: Skin appears white, blotchy, and/or blue. The underlying tissue is hard and cold to the touch.

**FACT**: Exposure too brief to affect the skin of the face or hands may still damage delicate tissues, such as the eyes.

## What are the emergency treatment steps and PPE for cryogenic liquids?

The effects of cryogenic liquid on skin are rapid. Minimal risk is achieved by wearing proper Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).

**Required PPE for Cryogenic Handling**:
- Rain slickers
- Face shields and goggles
- Hard hat
- Rubber coated gloves
- Rubber boots

**Emergency Action Steps for Cryogenic Burns**:
1. **Immediately flush** the affected areas with clean, cool water.
2. **Wrap** the affected areas with loose, clean bandages.
3. **DO NOT** rub the affected areas (rubbing increases tissue damage).
4. **Seek immediate medical assistance.**

## How is risk calculated for LNG vapor clouds?

The maximum vapor cloud fire hazard area is estimated by calculating downwind dispersion to the **Lower Flammable Limit (LFL)** and crosswind dispersion to **1/2 LFL** at low wind speeds.

**Assumptions for Injury**:
- Only the **outdoor population** within flammable boundaries is assumed to be injured due to thermal radiation, direct flame contact, clothing fires, or inhalation of hot combustion products.
- People **inside buildings** at the time of a flash fire are generally assumed not to be injured, as they can escape before fire penetrates the structure from the outside.

## What are Rollover and Rapid Phase Transition (RPT)?

- **Rollover**: Occurs when LNG of multiple densities layers (stratifies) in a tank. As the lower layer heats up and its density changes, a violent mixing (rollover) occurs. This can generate more vapor than the tank’s pressure control devices (PSVs, vents) can handle, leading to structural failure.
- **Rapid Phase Transition (RPT)**: A physical explosion occurring when LNG is released on water and vaporizes too quickly. It can also occur if large amounts of water are introduced to an LNG pool.
